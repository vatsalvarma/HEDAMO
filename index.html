Producer-Declared Product Disclosures

Hedamo provides a calm, structured interface for producers to disclose product information transparently. All information presented is producer-reported and not verified or certified.

More About Hedamo:

Hedamo is a structured disclosure platform designed to collect, organize, and present producer-declared information about products. Its core purpose is to enable manufacturers or producers to openly disclose details about their products—such as attributes, processes, materials, and supporting evidence—in a standardized and transparent format. Hedamo acts as a central information layer, where product-related disclosures can be accessed by different stakeholders. The system does not judge, verify, or certify the accuracy of the information. Instead, it focuses on making disclosures clear, consistent, and accessible, allowing users to understand what producers are claiming about their products.

Hedamo is explicitly not a certification body, verification authority, approval mechanism, or compliance system.

It does not verify whether the information provided by producers is true or accurate.
It does not certify products against any standard.
It does not approve, validate, endorse, or guarantee any product or claim.
It does not replace regulatory or third-party verification processes.


These boundaries are fundamental to the system. Hedamo intentionally avoids taking institutional authority over product claims. All responsibility for the disclosed information remains with the producer. This distinction is critical because misleading language or interface cues could incorrectly suggest that Hedamo validates or stands behind the disclosed information, which the system is designed to avoid. The value of Hedamo lies in structured transparency. By standardizing how information is disclosed, the platform reduces ambiguity, improves comparability across products, and enables informed decision-making without acting as an authority on correctness or compliance.

Hedamo is explicitly NOT a certification body, verification authority, compliance system, or approval mechanism. It does not validate the accuracy of information provided by producers. It does not certify products as meeting specific standards. It does not approve or endorse products in any capacity. This distinction is fundamental to the system's design and must be clearly reflected in all language, interface design, and user communication.

The system operates on a principle of "disclosure over verification." Producers enter information about their products, attach supporting evidence when available, and publish these disclosures for various stakeholders to review. The value lies in structured transparency, not in third-party validation. Any user interface you create must honor this philosophical foundation—using language that emphasizes producer responsibility for disclosed information rather than suggesting institutional authority or endorsement.
